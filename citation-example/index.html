Chamomile

Matricaria chamomilla

Overview

Chamomile is one of the most ancient medicinal herbs known to mankind. It is a member of the Asteraceae family and has been valued for centuries for its medicinal properties. 1 The herb has been traditionally used to treat various ailments including digestive issues, sleep problems, and skin conditions. 2

There are two common varieties of chamomile: German chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla) and Roman chamomile (Chamaemelum nobile). Both varieties contain similar beneficial compounds and are used interchangeably in many herbal preparations. 1

Traditional Uses

Historically, chamomile has been used in traditional medicine systems across Europe, Asia, and North Africa. Ancient Egyptians dedicated the herb to their gods and used it to treat fevers. 3 In medieval Europe, it was one of the nine sacred herbs mentioned in the Lacnunga, an Old English medical text. 3

Active Compounds

The pharmacological effects of chamomile are attributed to several bioactive compounds found in the essential oils and extracts. The primary active constituents include terpenoids (such as bisabolol and chamazulene) and flavonoids (including apigenin, quercetin, and luteolin). 1 Apigenin, in particular, has been studied for its ability to bind to specific receptors in the brain that may promote sedation and reduce anxiety. 4

Potential Benefits

Research suggests that chamomile may help with various health conditions. Studies have shown that chamomile extract may help reduce anxiety symptoms in individuals with generalized anxiety disorder. 4 Clinical trials have also investigated its potential effects on sleep quality, with some evidence suggesting it may improve sleep outcomes. 5

Additionally, chamomile has demonstrated anti-inflammatory and antioxidant properties in laboratory studies. 1 Topical application of chamomile has been studied for various skin conditions, including eczema and wound healing. 6

Safety & Side Effects

Chamomile is generally considered safe when consumed in amounts commonly found in teas and foods. 2 However, some individuals may experience allergic reactions, particularly those who are allergic to plants in the daisy family (Asteraceae/Compositae). 2

Chamomile may interact with blood-thinning medications, sedatives, and certain other drugs. Pregnant women should exercise caution and consult healthcare providers before using chamomile supplements, as high doses may stimulate uterine contractions. 7

When to See a Doctor

Seek immediate medical attention if you experience:

  • Severe allergic reactions (difficulty breathing, swelling of face or throat, severe rash)
  • Unusual bleeding or bruising (especially if taking blood thinners)
  • Severe digestive issues after consumption
  • Any symptoms that worsen or do not improve

Always consult a healthcare professional before using herbal supplements if you:

  • Are pregnant, nursing, or planning to become pregnant
  • Are taking prescription medications (especially blood thinners or sedatives)
  • Have a chronic health condition
  • Are scheduled for surgery
  • Have allergies to plants in the daisy family
